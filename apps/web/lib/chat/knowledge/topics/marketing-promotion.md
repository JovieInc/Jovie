# Marketing & Promotion

Marketing and promotion should create repeated chances for the right people to notice, remember, and return to the music. The job is not to be everywhere. The job is to be coherent and repeatable.

## Release Strategy and Timing

### Release Frequency

For most independent artists, consistent releases are easier to market than long gaps followed by one big push. Singles usually create more learning loops than albums because each release gives you a fresh shot at content, pitching, outreach, and audience testing.

The exact cadence depends on genre, team capacity, and catalog depth. A steady singles cadence usually beats waiting too long for a perfect album cycle. Pick a pace you can actually sustain.

### Planning Timeline

Start the campaign while the release is being finished, not after it is delivered. Build the content plan, artwork variations, video ideas, pre-save assets, and outreach list before launch week.

Always think one release ahead. Marketing works better when momentum can continue instead of stopping after the first spike.

## Digital Streaming Platform Strategy

### Playlist Submission

Submit early enough that editors and systems have time to process the release. Exact lead-time requirements can vary by distributor and platform, so treat any single deadline as guidance, not a law of physics.

When pitching, be specific about what the song is, who it is for, and why now. Avoid generic hype.

### Platform Optimization

Claim and maintain your artist profiles. Keep images, bios, links, and featured items current. A strong profile helps convert discovery into repeat listening.

If short-form preview or sound-selection tools are available on a platform, choose the most compelling clip rather than defaulting to the intro.

## Short-Form Video and Social Media

### Strategy

Short-form content works best when it feels native to your identity. Performance clips, studio moments, story-based context, and recurring formats usually outperform random trend-chasing.

Make it easy for a new viewer to understand:

- who you are
- what the song feels like
- why they should care now

### Content Production

Batch production helps. Capture multiple assets in one session, then cut them into different lengths and hooks. Consistency matters more than perfection.

Do not force a content style that clashes with the artist. The goal is recognizable signal, not generic creator behavior.

## Paid Marketing and Advertising

### Getting Started

Start with small tests, strong creative, and one clear objective. Examples:

- drive pre-saves before release
- drive streams during release week
- retarget warm listeners after launch

Do not spread a small budget across too many platforms at once.
For pre-release campaigns, the cleanest first conversion target is usually a pre-save or another simple warm-audience action, not cold stream volume on day one.
If the total budget is small, prioritize short-form content and a focused Meta or Instagram test over niche platform ad products. Tiny budgets usually go further when the creative can also work organically.

### Small Budget Rule of Thumb

If you only have around $200, keep the plan simple:

- make social content that can work organically on TikTok, Reels, and Shorts
- use a small paid test to support the strongest piece of content or the pre-save link
- keep enough budget for release week follow-up instead of spending everything at once

Do not burn the whole budget on a single named DSP ad product unless you already have strong evidence it fits your audience.

### Campaign Measurement

Judge paid activity by whether it moves quality signals such as saves, repeat listeners, follow behavior, or downstream catalog listening. Cheap clicks alone are not enough.

If cost is high and engagement is weak, fix the creative or audience fit before increasing spend.

## Fan Engagement and Community Building

### Direct Audience Channels

Owned channels matter because platform reach changes constantly. Email, text, Discord, or other direct channels can work if you actually maintain them. Use the channel you can keep alive consistently.

### Pre-Save Campaigns

Pre-saves can help when they are part of a broader release story. Use them as one conversion step, not the entire campaign. If you collect fan contact info through a pre-save flow, make sure the follow-up value is real.

## Press and Public Relations

Press is most useful when it sharpens the narrative around a release, milestone, or scene position. Outreach works better when the angle is clear and specific than when it is framed as “please cover my song.”

Relationships matter more than blast volume. Start with outlets, writers, and curators who plausibly fit the music.

## Data and Analytics

Use audience and engagement data to decide:

- which content angles deserve more repetition
- which territories are showing real traction
- which releases convert discovery into repeat behavior

Look for patterns across multiple signals rather than drawing big conclusions from one spike.

## Key Takeaways

- choose a release cadence you can actually sustain
- prepare the campaign before launch week
- focus on coherent short-form formats instead of random trend chasing
- test paid marketing with one objective at a time
- measure promotion by whether it drives intent and repeat behavior, not just exposure
