# Distribution Basics

Understanding how to get your music from creation to listeners is fundamental to building a sustainable career as an independent artist. Distribution involves far more than simply uploading files—it requires mastering metadata, identifiers, royalty structures, and platform relationships that determine whether you get paid properly for your work.

## Essential Music Industry Terminology

**Distributors** serve as the bridge between artists and digital service providers, ensuring recordings are properly uploaded, tracked, monetized, and that royalties flow to the correct rightsholders. They maximize reach by making music available worldwide through appropriate digital and physical channels.

**Digital Service Providers (DSPs)** are streaming platforms like Spotify or online stores that deliver digital audio to listeners and pay royalties to rightsholders based on usage.

**Metadata** encompasses all information embedded in digital music files, including visible elements like track title, artist, and album name, plus backend data such as release year, label, producer, and identifiers. Getting this information correct during initial upload is crucial since it drives accounting and ensures proper royalty distribution.

**Royalties** are payments made to rightsholders when their intellectual property is used. Each track use can generate multiple royalty types: some apply to compositions (mechanical and performance royalties), others to sound recordings (recording royalties). These flow to different parties—record labels, music publishers, and collective management organizations—depending on territory and rights involved.

## Critical Identifiers and Codes

**ISRC (International Standard Recording Code)** is a 12-character alphanumeric string that uniquely identifies any sound recording or music video. Like ISBNs for books, no two ISRCs are identical. This "digital fingerprint" should remain permanent regardless of who distributes the track. Ensure whoever issues your ISRC codes grants them to you in perpetuity.

**UPC (Universal Product Code)** is a unique 12-14 digit code identifying products in digital and physical stores. While ISRCs apply to individual tracks, UPCs apply at the album level. The same album on vinyl, CD, cassette, and digital will each have separate UPCs.

**ISWC (International Standard Musical Work Code)** globally identifies musical compositions (the songs themselves), not recordings. It identifies the composition, songwriters, composers, and publishers.

Five key codes form essential metadata: IPI identifies the songwriter/composer, ISWC identifies the musical work, ISRC identifies the specific recording, IPN identifies performers, and ISNI links these codes together.

## Rights and Copyright Framework

**Compositions** are the underlying intellectual property—melody, harmony, lyrics, and structure—that exist independently of any specific recording. **Recordings** (also called sound recordings or masters) are the fixed capture of musical performances, representing the actual audio listeners hear.

**P Line** (℗) indicates legal rights held for a sound recording and identifies the owner. **C Line** (©) indicates copyright in creative works other than sound recordings, such as cover art, liner notes, or the underlying composition itself. These appear separately even when the same entity owns both rights.

**Mechanical royalties** are paid to songwriters, composers, and publishers when their composition is reproduced—originally on piano rolls and vinyl, now including streaming and downloads. **Performance royalties** are paid when compositions are performed or broadcast publicly, including streaming, radio, TV, live concerts, and public venues.

**Recording royalties** are paid to artists, record labels, and other rightsholders for sound recording use, typically earned from streaming, downloads, or sales.

## Royalty Collection Infrastructure

**Collective Management Organizations (CMOs)** manage rights and collect royalties for songwriters, composers, publishers, and other rightsholders. They license music use to businesses, broadcasters, streaming services, and venues, then distribute generated royalties to members. CMOs include Performance Rights Organizations (PROs) and mechanical agencies.

**Performance Rights Organizations (PROs)** ensure songwriters, composers, and publishers receive performance royalties when musical works are broadcast, streamed, downloaded, or played publicly. They license music use, collect fees, and distribute payments to members.

**Mechanical agencies** issue mechanical licenses for song use, collect mechanical royalties from labels, platforms, and services, and distribute payments to appropriate songwriters and publishers.

**Unclaimed royalties** are funds collected by CMOs but unmatched to rightful owners due to missing or incomplete metadata. These are held temporarily while efforts are made to identify correct recipients. If unclaimed after a set period (varying by country and organization), funds are typically distributed pro rata among affiliated members.

## Platform Access and Optimization

**Claiming artist profiles** on platforms like Spotify, Apple Music, Amazon Music, and Deezer gives you control over your page, access to promotional tools, insights, and analytics unavailable otherwise. This demonstrates platform engagement and allows customization that helps fans understand your brand.

**Spotify for Artists access** can be obtained by claiming an artist profile, getting instant access through preferred distributors, or joining teams before first release. Labels and distributors can create teams to view entire catalogs matching their metadata.

**Release timing and pitching** requires submitting information 3-4 weeks before release for optimal consideration. Spotify's playlist submission tool requires at least 7 days advance notice for algorithmic playlist eligibility (Release Radar, Discover Weekly). Missing this window means tracks won't appear in these playlists until weeks 2-4 after release.

## Metadata Best Practices

Accurate metadata prevents revenue loss and ensures proper attribution. Common mistakes include using special characters in artist names (dollar signs, hyphens, non-Latin characters) that systems may not handle properly, potentially costing thousands in lost royalties.

**Split sheets** document who contributed what during songwriting and each party's royalty percentage entitlement. Establishing these before release prevents future disputes and ensures proper payment distribution.

**Label copy** includes all information accompanying sound recordings: artist name, song title, ISRC, composer, publisher, and rights holder. Without this information, proper royalty distribution is impossible.

## Streaming Platform Strategies

**Pre-saves** and engaged streams (where listeners proactively choose to play tracks) are favored algorithmically over lean-back streams from playlists. Saves to user libraries provide additional positive signals to platform algorithms.

**Consistency** in release schedules helps platforms and fans. Know what comes after your current release to capitalize on momentum when tracks gain traction.

**Territory focus** typically starts with home markets for emerging artists before expanding internationally, as DSPs generally focus on building established local audiences first.

## Key Takeaways

- Secure permanent ownership of your ISRC codes from whoever issues them
- Submit complete, accurate metadata using simple character sets in artist names
- Claim all available artist profiles and optimize them consistently across platforms
- Establish split sheets before releasing collaborative work
- Submit releases 3-4 weeks in advance with playlist pitching at least 7 days before release
- Join both PROs (for performance royalties) and mechanical collection organizations (for mechanical royalties) to capture all revenue streams
- Focus on building engaged audiences through pre-saves and direct fan interaction rather than passive playlist placement alone
- Maintain consistent release schedules and have follow-up material ready when tracks gain momentum
