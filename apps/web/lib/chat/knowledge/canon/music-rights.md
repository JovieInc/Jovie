# Music Rights

Understanding music rights is essential for independent artists to protect their work and maximize their income from every use of their music.

## Core Rights and Copyright Protection

Music involves two distinct copyrights that generate separate revenue streams. The **composition** encompasses the underlying musical work—melody, harmony, lyrics, and structure—while the **recording** (also called a sound recording or master) captures the specific audio performance that listeners hear. Each carries its own copyright protection and generates different types of royalties.

Copyright notices reflect this distinction. The **P Line** (℗) identifies ownership of sound recording rights and appears with the year of first release and owner's name. The **C Line** (©) indicates copyright in other creative works, including cover art, liner notes, and the underlying composition itself. These symbols appear separately even when the same entity owns both rights.

## Essential Identification Codes

Five key identifiers form the backbone of music rights management. The **ISRC** (International Standard Recording Code) is a unique 12-character alphanumeric string that identifies each sound recording or music video—functioning like a VIN for cars or ISBN for books. This digital fingerprint must remain permanent regardless of who distributes the track, so ensure whoever issues your ISRC grants it to you in perpetuity.

The **ISWC** (International Standard Musical Work Code) identifies the musical composition itself, along with its songwriters, composers, and publishers. The **IPI** identifies individual songwriters and composers, while the **IPN** identifies performers. The **ISNI** links these four codes together. Additionally, the **UPC** (Universal Product Code) is a 12-14 digit identifier that applies at the album or product level—each format (vinyl, CD, cassette, digital) receives its own UPC.

## Royalty Types and Collection

**Performance royalties** flow to songwriters, composers, and publishers when their compositions are performed or broadcast publicly—including streaming, downloads, radio airplay, TV broadcasts, live concerts, and music played in public spaces. These royalties are collected and distributed through Performance Rights Organizations (PROs).

**Mechanical royalties** are paid whenever a composition is reproduced, whether through physical sales (vinyl, CDs), digital downloads, or streaming. The term originates from early mechanical reproduction methods but now applies to all forms of reproduction.

**Recording royalties** go to artists, record labels, and other rightsholders for use of the sound recording itself. These are typically earned from streaming, downloads, or sales and are collected by labels and distributors.

**Synchronization royalties** are generated when music is paired with moving images in films, TV shows, commercials, video games, or short-form content. Each sync use requires a special license.

## Collective Management Organizations

**Collective Management Organizations (CMOs)** manage rights and collect royalties on behalf of creators. They license music use to businesses, broadcasters, streaming services, and venues, then distribute the generated royalties to their members. CMOs track music usage, manage metadata, and ensure creators receive compensation when their works are performed, broadcast, streamed, or reproduced.

**Performance Rights Organizations (PROs)** are a specific type of CMO that handle performance royalties. In the US, songwriters cannot collect mechanical royalties simply by joining a PRO, nor can they collect performance royalties by only joining a mechanical collection organization—both memberships are required for complete royalty collection.

**Mechanical agencies** issue mechanical licenses, collect mechanical royalties from labels and platforms, and distribute payments to appropriate songwriters and publishers.

## Critical Registration and Metadata Requirements

Proper registration with relevant CMOs is mandatory—if you're not registered, you won't get paid. **Metadata**—the collection of information embedded in digital music files—is crucial for royalty distribution. This includes visible information like track title, artist, and album name, plus back-end data like release year, label, producer, and identifier codes.

Metadata accuracy is critical because typos equal missing payments. Essential details include correctly spelled song titles and writer names, agreed-upon splits, and all co-writers' information including their IPI numbers. When registering works, include as much accurate information as possible to ensure faster, more accurate matching and payment.

## Split Sheets and Collaboration

**Split sheets** document who contributed what during songwriting and each party's royalty percentage entitlement. These must be completed immediately during writing sessions—not months later when details become unclear. For collaborative works, all writers must be properly credited and registered, as missing or conflicting information can stop payments entirely.

## Territory and Licensing

**Territory** defines the geographical area where specific music rights and licenses apply. Most licensing agreements specify where music can be used, streamed, or sold. If a song isn't licensed for a particular territory, it cannot be made available there. When no territorial restrictions exist, music can be distributed worldwide.

## Unclaimed Royalties and Common Problems

**Unclaimed royalties** result from missing or incomplete metadata, spelling errors, conflicting registrations, or unregistered songs. These funds are held temporarily while efforts are made to identify rightful owners. If they remain unclaimed after a set period (varying by country and organization), funds are typically distributed pro rata among the organization's members.

Common mistakes include releasing music before splits are negotiated, failing to register songs with appropriate organizations, and entering publishing deals without understanding their terms. Some distributors still don't properly handle mechanical royalties, potentially sitting on funds that should flow to songwriters.

## Key Takeaways

- Register with both a PRO and mechanical collection organization to capture all royalty types
- Complete split sheets immediately during writing sessions, not months later
- Ensure metadata accuracy—typos directly result in lost payments
- Obtain permanent ownership of your ISRC codes
- Register songs immediately upon creation, not after they become successful
- Join CMOs early in your career to maximize their ability to help you
- Verify that your distributor properly handles and pays out mechanical royalties
- Check CMO databases regularly to confirm your registrations are correct
- Consider multiple society memberships if you work internationally
- Understand that each use of your music can generate multiple royalty types flowing through different collection systems
