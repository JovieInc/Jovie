# What's Next for Music

The problem with the music industry isn't talent. It's that independent artists do not release enough music to learn what works before they run out of money, time, or motivation.

Startups die before product-market fit. Artists quit before they learn what works.

And one of the biggest reasons is simple: you can't afford a team until you no longer desperately need one.

Building a music career requires a manager to coordinate, a publicist to pitch press, a radio plugger to work records, a designer for album art, a photographer for press shots, an editor for lyric videos, someone to run socials, someone to handle booking. Each person holds a fragment of context, does their piece, and passes it along.

The team you need at 1,000 monthly listeners is the same team you need at 100,000. You just can't pay for it yet. And by the time you can, you've already lost years of momentum doing it yourself — badly, slowly, or not at all.

That's the game. And it hasn't changed in decades.

Until now.

---

## The shift

The numbers are bonkers. Global recorded music revenues hit $31.7 billion in 2025 — the eleventh consecutive year of growth. Spotify alone paid out $11 billion, the largest annual payment to music from any retailer in history. Independent artists and labels now account for half of all royalties. More than 13,800 artists earned over $100,000 from Spotify alone last year — more artists making a living from streaming than ever got stocked on record store shelves at the height of the CD era.

And live music is bigger than it has ever been. Live Nation did $25 billion in 2025. 159 million fans. 55,000 shows. Average ticket: $144, up 31% in five years. The more AI floods platforms with generated content, the more fans crave the real thing. Touring isn't dying. It's the fastest-growing revenue line in the industry.

Here's the tension: as creation gets cheaper and the market grows, everything *after* creation gets harder. More releases. More platforms to update. More fans to notify. More press kits. More social content. More booking inquiries. More tours to coordinate. More collaborators to manage.

Whether you record in a studio or generate a track with AI, the post-creation workload is identical — and it's growing. AI made the music. Nobody is making it easy to do everything else.

Independent artists — half of all streaming royalties now — need album art designed, press shots retouched, lyric videos cut, radio pluggers working their singles, blogs pitched, social content scheduled, booking inquiries answered. The work is real. The budget isn't there.

So they do it themselves, or they don't do it at all. The average independent artist releases 1-3 songs a year. Not because they can only write 1-3 songs — because the work around each release is so heavy that each one becomes a months-long project.

Every release is a test. You ship a song, the world tells you what it thinks, you learn, and you make the next one. If one artist ships 10 songs and another ships three, the first artist gets 10 learning loops. More releases means faster learning.

Flip that. If an artist could release a song every week — with every release automatically getting smart links, fan notifications, playlist pitching, and audience capture — that's 52 shots on goal per year. Each one gets a full learning loop. Even if 9 out of 10 bomb, you end the year with signal. That's how you build demand: not by marketing harder on fewer releases, but by removing the friction that keeps release velocity artificially low.

Coordination isn't downstream of demand. It's the bottleneck that's choking it.

## Context changes everything

When you release a song, an intelligent system should already know your discography, your streaming trajectory, your audience demographics, your collaborators, your tour history, your fan engagement patterns. It should know your feature artist hasn't been linked to this release on any platform yet. It should know your fans in Nashville engage 3x more than your fans in LA. It should know your last single got picked up by a playlist curator who responds to DMs on Tuesdays.

None of that requires a manager. It requires *context*.

The information needed to run an artist's career exists — scattered across Spotify for Artists, Instagram analytics, email inboxes, Google Docs, text threads, and the memories of whoever happens to be managing things this month. When the manager changes, the context walks out the door. I know this because I fired my manager and had to update every single platform, every contact, every relationship from scratch.

No single platform owns this picture. Spotify knows your streams but not your booking inquiries. Instagram knows your followers but not your press history. Your distributor knows your catalog but not your fan engagement. The value isn't in any one silo — it's in unifying them. The system that connects an artist's streaming data, audience behavior, business communications, and creative output into one context layer is the system that makes everything else possible.

Distributors won't build this — they're logistics companies optimized for throughput, not career intelligence. Spotify won't — they serve labels and artists simultaneously and can't take sides. Social platforms won't — they capture attention, not career context. Each incumbent owns one silo and has no incentive to unify across the others.

An AI agent without context is just a chatbot. An agent with deep context on both your work and your audience is a career engine.

Linear recently articulated this for software: the next system isn't designed around handoffs — it's designed around context and agents. The same shift is happening in music. It's even more overdue.

## From automation to orchestration

What we're building — what Jovie becomes — is the system where every artist gets a team from day one. Some of those team members are agents. Some are humans. The artist doesn't have to care which.

**Today, Jovie automates.** New release detected → smart links generated → fans captured → notifications sent → playlist pitch generated → collaborators linked. Zero artist input, zero configuration. Not agents that reason — automations that execute reliably because they already have the context. They work for free, and they never miss a release. These are your first team members.

**What we're building next: Jovie adds roles.** A booking inquiry arrives at your permanent Jovie email. An agent handles intake — asking the right questions, gathering the right information, only escalating when human judgment is needed. Your album art gets generated from your release mood and brand. Your press shot gets retouched. Your lyric video gets cut. Your radio plugger starts working the single. Each role is filled by an agent that has full context on your career — or by a human specialist you bring in, who inherits that same context instantly.

**Where this leads: Jovie scales the team.** At 1,000 listeners, your team is mostly agents. At 100,000 listeners, you start adding human specialists — a real publicist, a real booking agent — and the agents augment their capacity. The context layer means your human publicist doesn't start from scratch. They inherit everything: your press history, your audience demographics, what pitches landed, what didn't. The humans get better because the system already knows the artist.

The artist sees everything. Every action is logged, every decision is explainable, every automation can be overridden. In an industry where artists have been burned by opaque intermediaries for decades, transparency isn't a feature — it's the foundation of trust.

The distance between finishing a song and having a full team launch it into the world — that distance collapses. Not because you replaced the team. Because you finally got one.

## What this means

The music industry was designed for scarcity: scarce studio time, scarce distribution, scarce audience attention. Intermediaries managed the scarcity.

We're in an era of abundance: abundant creation tools, abundant distribution, abundant ways to reach listeners. And as AI floods every platform with generated content, the premium on *authenticity* — live performance, real artistry, genuine fan relationships — grows. Touring just posted its best year in history. Fans are paying more than ever for real experiences. The artists who cut through the noise and connect with real audiences will build the careers that matter.

What's scarce now is *coordination* — the ability to take a finished piece of work, reach the right audience, book the right shows, and do everything needed to give it a life in the world.

Jovie is the shared system for an artist's career. It captures the context that makes both agents and humans more effective: what you've released, who's listening, what's working, what needs to happen next. It gives every artist a team — agents first, humans when you're ready — and ensures no context is ever lost when the team grows or changes.

Every artist who joins makes the system smarter. Every release deepens the context. Every fan interaction refines the understanding.

90 million streams taught me exactly which parts of a music career are creative decisions and which parts are manual work. The creative decisions should be human. Everything else should be automatic. The point is to help artists release more music, learn faster, and find what works before they quit.

This is what's next.
